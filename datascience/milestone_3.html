Austin Nguyen and Elena Yang

Project Goals¶

The goal of this project is to understand the relation of chess openings with different variables such as the outcome of the match, user ratings and game time formats. The optimal openings have been a controversy discussion and we believe the differences in data resulting from variations in openings would provide better insight. The question that we will attempt to answer is how much of a factor openings have in terms of winning with the different variables presented.

Project Dataset¶

The first dataset we plan to work with is taken from lichess. Each unit of observation is a player on from "Lilchess Teams" uniquely identified by their usernames. This information includes data such as the time the game was played, the users that participated, the exact moves that were played; this means the opening can be directly inferred and placed within the data.

The average rating of the dataset is around 1600; although the quality of the games are not Grand Master level, the average rating reflects that of the slightly-above average player which allows the information to be a bit more useful to an average player. This is important since the information when one attempts to find a "useful" opening would come from a scenario where a GrandMaster would use the opening, which may or may not be as useful to the average player, i.e., the larger demographic.

In addition, although the average is around 1600, it takes into account a variety of ratings which means we are able to also look at different openings used for the range of ratings provided.

While this information is useful, it could be beneficial to increase the sample size of games as this is a relatively small sample of 20,000 observations. It would also be interesting to include some higher rated games to see a difference in trends depending on the ratings of the players.

Our second dataset was taking from the lichess database, where took every rated game from January 2013. Since this is from a different timeframe than our first dataset we do not need to check for any overlapping or duplicate games. We then combined the two datasets to have much more games, specifically around 100,000.

With the introduction of the new dataset, the average rating is still around 1600, which is expected since that is the average rating of the site. This means that although we have many more games, overall the average level of the games that were added won't stray too far from the previous dataset.

One thing that differed from the two datasets is that for the first dataset, the Opening Name was already in the dataset, however for the second one, there was no name. So to fix this, we included a third dataset that was datascraped, and allowed us to include a name for the openings in the second dataset.

ETL (Extraction, Transform, and Load)¶

We have loaded our dataset found on Lichess to the GitHub. At first, we thought that in order to have a table that is slightly easier to read, we would remove the "moves" column which reflects later moves made in the match, since the first moves would be known through the opening which is what we are mainly concerned with. However, the analyzing the specific moves gives us more weight to what goes on the game, as there are multiple openings with the first moves for example e4-e5.

Looking at the data set, there is a rated column indicating False if the game was not rated and True otherwise. For the purpose of this analysis we will focus on rated games only. The amount of variables that are introduced that could vary are so substantial that it just makes it impossible to answer our question of the effects of openings when considering unrated games. For example, take an unrated game between a player with a rating of 1600 and a player with an 800 rating. Regardless of the opening used, the higher-rated person would likely win. Another scenario could be two people playing an unserious game where they do not care about the outcome. About 83% of this data set is rated games.

Opening Length vs General Opening¶

The opening_ply column tells us how many moves are in the opening part of the game. We can say games where the opening has more moves, e.g., a 5-move opening game vs a 2-move opening game, the opening is a more closed game state since more moves are predetermined by the opening in which case the opening would have more of an impact.

However, one opening can have multiple variations and then multiple opening_ply values. For example, Alekhine's Defense takes 1. e4 Nf6 2. e5 Nd5 3. d4, whereas Alekhine's Defense, Modern is 1. e4 Nf6 2. e5 Nd5 3. d4 d6 4. Nf3. We will analyze the datasets by both grouping the general openings together, and by the opening_ply value provided in the datasets.

When we group the dataset by the opening_ply column. Since we want to give more weight to those games with a larger opening_ply value, we will compute the weighted average of the winrates among all the games with a certain opening_ply value.
